sicut cor ita cervus

Oct 30, 2017

I knew I belonged in Harvard Glee Club when we sang a TTBB arrangement of Palestrina's Sicut cervus at the first rehearsal freshman year. The text is an adaptation of Psalm 42:1 Sicut cervus desiderat ad fontes aquarum, ita desiderat anima mea ad te, Deus. The sheet music we were using had a typo in the English translation, using heart instead of hart2 as the translation for cervus. Interestingly, just as hart and heart sound similar, so do cervus and the Latin word for heart, cor,3 and even more interestingly, this is not a coincidence.

The two words in each language sound similar because they are derived from words that sound similar in Proto-Indo-European. Heart and cor are derived from PIE *ḱḗr (oblique stem *ḱr̥d-), which is well-attested across the many branches of Indo-European; another familiar example is Greek kardiá.4 The relationship between heart and cor is unexceptional to anyone with exposure to PIE. Less commonly known is the corresponding relationship between hart and cervus, both rarer words in their respective languages. Hart is derived from PIE *ḱerh₂- ("horn"),5 which is also the source of cervus. We see that *ḱḗr and *ḱerh₂- sound similar, and by regularity of sound change,6 words derived from similar-sounding roots in the proto-language will continue to sound similar in daughter languages, which resulted in the confusion in the English translation of Sicut cervus.

  1. Psalm 41:2 in the Vulgate. ↩

  2. hart n. An adult male deer, especially a red deer over five years old. Oxford English Dictionary ↩

  3. cor, cordis. From which we derive the words core, accord, cordial, etc. via French. ↩

  4. καρδιά (kardiá). From which we derive the root cardio- and therefore words like cardiology and cardiovascular, and other words like tachycardia. ↩

  5. *ḱerh₂- ("horn"). From which we also derive the English words horn; flugelhorn from German; cornucopia, cornea, cervix from Latin and unicorn, carrot, cerebral, corner from Latin via French; keratin, rhinoceros, triceratops from Greek and cranium from Greek via Latin. Online Etymology Dictionary ↩

  6. The notable sound changes here are PIE *ḱ resulting in English h and Latin k, often spelled c, which later palatalized before the front vowels i and e. Another sound change is PIE *d resulting in English t and Latin d. ↩
