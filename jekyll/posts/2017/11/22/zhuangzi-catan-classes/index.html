Zhuangzi, Catan, and Classes

Nov 22, 2017

Thanksgiving provides me with some well-needed rest before I jump back in to finish the semester, giving me time to reflect a little bit and collect some random thoughts.

Zhuangzi's passage on the happiness of fish discusses the relativity of knowledge: How does Zhuangzi know whether the fish are happy? But Huizi cannot know what Zhuangzi knows… the cycle continues. Zhuangzi finishes with a play on the word 安, which can mean either "how" or "where." Last night, I read a blog post that focused on that last line, and I found both the post and its comment chain enjoyable to peruse, comparing relativity of perspective to relativity of location (hence 安 as "whence") and the joy of philosophy to the playfulness of fish.

A couple weeks ago, a game of Catan ended in an interesting way: it was Will's turn, and should Will end his turn it would be mine, and I would immediately build a settlement with the resources in my hand and win the game. Andrew, who almost has the resources to win himself, recognized that if he gave all of his resources to Will, he could enable Will to win before me. Wishing to prolong the game, he threatened to do so unless I forfeited the resources in my hand that would have allowed me to win. I gave up my resources and was forced to roll on my turn; I still got lucky with my roll, but I wonder if Andrew could have strong-armed me further than he had done. Perhaps the game could have been prolonged further if Andrew, Will, and I had entered an agreement to give all of our resources to Rowen, who was far away in last place. My game of Catan presented a similar situation to Risk, in which a player might threaten to retaliate in full force against any invader, essentially sacking the current game to win the metagame. This also leads me to wonder how much negative expected value I would be willing to take on to prevent my opponents from winning high positive expected value.

Classes are finally coming to a close. All classes build up in nature; the end material rests on the foundation laid in the beginning. Yet I think I stand more to learn at the end of this semester than at the end of my previous semesters: Math 114 is entering Fourier analysis and exploring some familiar results from complex analysis, like the mean-value property of harmonic functions, in a real analysis context (perhaps its original context); Stat 210 is covering the Central Limit Theorem in more rigor; ER 18 is synthesizing all of the thinkers we'd covered in the class; USW 35 is now proposing and discussing solutions to the problems in our education system. Everything is coming together, but it's difficult to keep track of everything, and truly, tempus fugit.
