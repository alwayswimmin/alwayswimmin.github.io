Egorychev, Part 2

Jul 15, 2017

Ross sent me an interesting math problem last month, and I detailed my attempt at a solution in a blog post a little more than a week ago. With his substantial help, I was finally able to make some headway, and the following solution is much more a recreation of his rather than my own.

Let \(0 \le a < 1\) and \(n \in \mathbb{Z}^+\), and define \[ I_n = \left( 1-a^2 \right)^{n-1/2} \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{d\theta}{\left( 1 + a\cos\theta \right)^n}. \] Express \(I_n\) as a polynomial in \(a\).

As I mentioned in my earlier post, the reader should mind two key insights using complex analysis: the first, a treatment of \(\cos\theta\) I'd dealt with regularly, and the second, the Egorychev method, which I hadn't yet encountered. The beginning of the solution is much the same, so one who has already read my previous post on this problem can comfortably skip through the first steps.

We first get rid of the integral as quickly as possible and worry about simplifying later, applying a standard substitution of \(z = e^{i\theta}\), transforming the real integral into a complex one. We then have \(\cos\theta = \frac{1}{2} \left( z + \frac{1}{z} \right)\) and \(dz = iz \,d\theta\), and the integral becomes \[ \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{d\theta}{\left( 1 + a\cos\theta \right)^n} = \oint_{\lvert z \rvert=1} \frac{1}{iz} \frac{dz}{\left( 1 + \frac{a}{2} \left( z + \frac{1}{z} \right) \right)^n}. \] If \(a = 0\), \(I_n = 2\pi\) immediately; otherwise, we have \begin{align} &\phantom{ {}={} } \oint_{\lvert z \rvert=1} \frac{1}{iz} \frac{dz}{\left( 1 + \frac{a}{2} \left( z + \frac{1}{z} \right) \right)^n} \\ &= \frac{1}{i} \oint_{\lvert z \rvert=1} \frac{z^{n-1} \,dz}{\left( \frac{a}{2} z^2 + z + \frac{a}{2} \right)^n} \\ &= \frac{1}{i} \left( \frac{2}{a} \right)^n \oint_{\lvert z \rvert=1} \frac{z^{n-1} \,dz}{\left( \left( z - \frac{-1 - \sqrt{1-a^2}}{a} \right) \left( z - \frac{-1 + \sqrt{1-a^2}}{a} \right) \right)^n} \\ &= 2\pi \left( \frac{2}{a} \right)^n \frac{1}{(n-1)!} \left. \frac{d^{n-1}}{dz^{n-1}} \left( \frac{z^{n-1}}{\left( z - \frac{-1 - \sqrt{1-a^2}}{a} \right)^n } \right) \right\rvert_{z = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{1-a^2}}{a}}, \end{align} by Cauchy's integral formula. For the sake of simplifying notation, we substitute \(w = z - \frac{-1 - \sqrt{1-a^2}}{a}\), giving us \begin{align} &\phantom{ {}={} } 2\pi \left( \frac{2}{a} \right)^n \frac{1}{(n-1)!} \left. \frac{d^{n-1}}{dz^{n-1}} \left( \frac{z^{n-1}}{\left( z - \frac{-1 - \sqrt{1-a^2}}{a} \right)^n } \right) \right\rvert_{z = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{1-a^2}}{a}} \\ &= 2\pi \left( \frac{2}{a} \right)^n \frac{1}{(n-1)!} \left. \frac{d^{n-1}}{dw^{n-1}} \left( \frac{\left(w + \frac{-1 - \sqrt{1-a^2}}{a} \right)^{n-1}}{ w^n } \right) \right\rvert_{w = \frac{2\sqrt{1-a^2}}{a}} \\ &= 2\pi \left( \frac{2}{a} \right)^n \frac{1}{(n-1)!} \left. \frac{d^{n-1}}{dw^{n-1}} \left( \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k} \left( \frac{-1 - \sqrt{1-a^2}}{a} \right)^k w^{-k-1} \right) \right\rvert_{w = \frac{2\sqrt{1-a^2}}{a}} \\ &= 2\pi \left( \frac{2}{a} \right)^n \frac{1}{(n-1)!} \left. \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k} \frac{(-1)^{n-1} (n-1+k)!}{k!} \left( \frac{-1 - \sqrt{1-a^2}}{a} \right)^k w^{-k-n} \right\rvert_{w = \frac{2\sqrt{1-a^2}}{a}} \\ &= 2\pi \left( \frac{2}{a} \right)^n (-1)^{n-1} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{(n-1+k)!}{k!k!(n-1-k)!} \left( \frac{-1 - \sqrt{1-a^2}}{a} \right)^k \left( \frac{2\sqrt{1-a^2}}{a} \right)^{-k-n} \\ &= 2\pi (-1)^{n-1} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{(n-1+k)!}{k!k!(n-1-k)!} \left( \frac{-1 - \sqrt{1-a^2}}{2} \right)^k \left( \sqrt{1-a^2} \right)^{-k-n}. \end{align} We then have \begin{align} I_n &= \left( \sqrt{1-a^2} \right)^{2n-1} 2\pi (-1)^{n-1} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{(n-1+k)!}{k!k!(n-1-k)!} \left( \frac{-1 - \sqrt{1-a^2}}{2} \right)^k \left( \sqrt{1-a^2} \right)^{-k-n} \\ &= 2\pi (-1)^{n-1} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{(n-1+k)!}{k!k!(n-1-k)!} \left( -\frac{1 + \sqrt{1-a^2}}{2} \right)^k \left( \sqrt{1-a^2} \right)^{n-1-k}. \end{align} Again, for the sake of simplifying notation, let \(x = \sqrt{1-a^2}\). Notice that there very well may be terms of odd powers of \(x\), so we have no indication yet that the resulting value will end up being a polynomial in \(a\). We then have \begin{align} &\phantom{ {}={} } 2\pi (-1)^{n-1} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{(n-1 + k)!}{k!k!(n-1 - k)!} \left( -\frac{1 + x}{2} \right)^k x^{n-1 - k} \\ &= 2\pi \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \sum_{j=0}^k \frac{(n-1 + k)!}{k! (n-1 - k)! j! (k - j)!} (-2)^{-k} x^{n-1 - j}. \end{align} This is a minor change from my previous solution. We expand \( \left( \frac{1 + x}{2} \right)^k = \sum_{j=0}^k \binom{k}{j} \left(\frac{x}{2}\right)^{k-j} \) rather than using \(j\) as the exponent; this avoids some annoying reindexing, as the resulting exponent of \(x\) in the double sum depends only on \(j\) rather than a combination. Swapping the sums gives \begin{align} &\phantom{ {}={} } 2\pi \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \sum_{j=0}^k \frac{(n-1 + k)!}{k! (n-1 - k)! j! (k - j)!} (-2)^{-k} x^{n-1 - j} \\ &= 2\pi \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} x^{n-1 - j} \sum_{k=j}^{n-1} \frac{(n-1 + k)!}{k! (n-1 - k)! j! (k - j)!} (-2)^{-k} \\ &= 2\pi \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} x^{n-1 - j} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1 + k}{k - j} \binom{n-1 + j}{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k} (-2)^{-k} \\ &= 2\pi \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} x^{n-1 - j} \binom{n-1 + j}{n-1} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1 + k}{k - j} \binom{n-1}{k} (-2)^{-k}, \end{align} where we extend the sum over \(k\) to start at \(0\), as terms where \(k < j\) are \(0\) anyway.

Here we apply the Egorychev method. Up until this point I'd been in a mad dash to get rid of the integral as quickly as possible, and then simplify as much as possible, but then I was blocked by a binomial sum I couldn't handle. The Egorychev method involves introducing complex contour integrals to simplify binomial identities, which seems a little backwards â€“ after all, didn't we just work so hard to get rid of a contour integral in the first place? Well, let's see what this method really does. Our main tool is the following identity: \[ \binom{s}{t} = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{\lvert z \rvert = \varepsilon} \frac{(1+z)^s \,dz}{z^{t+1}} \] There are other possible integrals we could use to express binomials, but this will suffice for this problem. My main intuition here for why we would ever want to substitute this is that binomials are nice in two ways:

  1. Each individual binomial is pretty arithmetically: \(\binom{s}{t} = \frac{s!}{t!(s-t)!}\).
  2. Binomials combine to be pretty algebraically: \(\sum_{t=0}^s \binom{s}{t}z^t = (1+z)^s\) is the simplest example, and we can think of generating functions more generally.

There are nice ways combinatorially to think about binomials using both these facts simultaneously, but here we're stuck with individual binomials in a sum that doesn't allow them to play nicely off of each other, especially because of this nasty \( (-2)^{n-1 - k} \) term. We'd like to be able to take advantage of property 2, which is what the Egorychev method affords us. Applying the method to the binomial \(\binom{n-1 + k}{k - j}\) gives us \begin{align} &\phantom{ {}={} } \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1 + k}{k - j} \binom{n-1}{k} (-2)^{-k} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi i} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \oint_{\lvert z \rvert = \varepsilon} \frac{(1+z)^{n-1 + k} \,dz}{z^{k - j + 1}} \binom{n-1}{k} (-2)^{-k} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{\lvert z \rvert = \varepsilon} \frac{(1+z)^{n-1}}{z^{-j + 1}} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k} \left( -\frac{1}{2z} - \frac{1}{2} \right)^k \,dz \\ &= \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{\lvert z \rvert = \varepsilon} \frac{(1+z)^{n-1}}{z^{n-1 - j + 1}} \left( -\frac{1}{2z} + \frac{1}{2} \right)^{n-1} \,dz \\ &= \frac{1}{2^{n-1}} \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{\lvert z \rvert = \varepsilon} \frac{\left( 1 - z^2 \right)^{n-1} \,dz}{z^{n-1 - j + 1}} \\ &= \begin{cases} 0 & 2 \not\mid n-1 - j \\ \frac{1}{2^{n-1}} \binom{n-1}{(n-1 - j)/2} (-1)^{(n-1 - j)/2} & 2 \mid n-1 - j. \end{cases} \end{align} Now let \(k' = \frac{n-1 - j}{2}\). We can then write \begin{align} &\phantom{ {}={} } 2\pi \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} x^{n-1 - j} \binom{n-1 + j}{n-1} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1 + k}{k - j} \binom{n-1}{k} (-2)^{-k} \\ &= \frac{2\pi}{2^{n-1}} \sum_{k'=0}^{n-1} x^{2k'} \binom{2(n-1) - 2k'}{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k'} (-1)^{k'} \\ &= \frac{2\pi}{2^{n-1}} \sum_{k'=0}^{n-1} \binom{2(n-1) - 2k'}{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k'} (-1)^{k'} \left( 1 - a^2 \right)^{k'}, \end{align} which is indeed a polynomial in \(a\). Expanding once more and swapping the sums, we have \begin{align} &\phantom{ {}={} } \frac{2\pi}{2^{n-1}} \sum_{k'=0}^{n-1} \binom{2(n-1) - 2k'}{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k'} (-1)^{k'} \left( 1 - a^2 \right)^{k'} \\ &= \frac{2\pi}{2^{n-1}} \sum_{k'=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \binom{2(n-1) - 2k'}{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k'} (-1)^{k'} \sum_{m=0}^{k'} \binom{k'}{m} (-1)^m a^{2m} \\ &= \frac{2\pi}{2^{n-1}} \sum_{m=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} (-1)^m a^{2m} \sum_{k'=m}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \binom{2(n-1) - 2k'}{n-1} \binom{n-1}{k'} \binom{k'}{m} (-1)^{k'} \\ &= \frac{2\pi}{2^{n-1}} \sum_{m=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} (-1)^m a^{2m} \sum_{k'=m}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \binom{2(n-1) - 2k'}{n-1} \binom{n-1}{m} \binom{n-1 - m}{k' - m} (-1)^{k'} \\ &= \frac{2\pi}{2^{n-1}} \sum_{m=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} (-1)^m a^{2m} \binom{n-1}{m} \sum_{k'=m}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \binom{2(n-1) - 2k'}{n-1} \binom{n-1 - m}{k' - m} (-1)^{k'}. \end{align} We now use the Egorychev method again to the binomial \(\binom{2(n-1) - 2k'}{n-1}\): \begin{align} &\phantom{ {}={} } \sum_{k'=m}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \binom{2(n-1) - 2k'}{n-1} \binom{n-1 - m}{k' - m} (-1)^{k'} \\ &= \frac{1}{2 \pi i} \sum_{k'=m}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \oint_{\lvert z \rvert = \varepsilon} \frac{(1+z)^{2(n-1) - 2k'} \,dz}{z^n} \binom{n-1 - m}{k' - m} (-1)^{k'} \\ &= \frac{1}{2 \pi i} (-1)^m \oint_{\lvert z \rvert = \varepsilon} \frac{1}{z^n} \sum_{k'=m}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \binom{n-1 - m}{k' - m} \left( 1 + 2z + z^2 \right)^{n-1 - k'} (-1)^{k' - m} \,dz \\ &= \frac{1}{2 \pi i} (-1)^m \oint_{\lvert z \rvert = \varepsilon} \frac{1}{z^n} \left( 2z + z^2 \right)^{n-1 - m} \,dz \\ &= \frac{1}{2 \pi i} (-1)^m \oint_{\lvert z \rvert = \varepsilon} \frac{(2 + z)^{n-1 - m} \,dz}{z^{m + 1}} \\ &= (-1)^m 2^{n-1 - 2m} \binom{n-1 - m}{m}. \end{align} Finishing, we have \begin{align} I_n &= \frac{2\pi}{2^{n-1}} \sum_{m=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} (-1)^m a^{2m} \binom{n-1}{m} \sum_{k'=m}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \binom{2(n-1) - 2k'}{n-1} \binom{n-1 - m}{k' - m} (-1)^{k'} \\ I_n &= \frac{2\pi}{2^{n-1}} \sum_{m=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} (-1)^m a^{2m} \binom{n-1}{m} (-1)^m 2^{n-1 - 2m} \binom{n-1 - m}{m} \\ I_n &= 2\pi \sum_{m=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor}\binom{n-1}{m} \binom{n-1 - m}{m} \left(\frac{a}{2}\right)^{2m}. \end{align} This end result is very nice. The general strategy in this problem centered around simplifying sums of the form \(\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{s}{t} \binom{n}{k} c^k\), where the first binomial was manipulated using Egorychev, and the resulting exponential combines with the second binomial to simplify the sum. The Wikipedia article on the Egorychev method highlights some examples of more complicated uses of the method, using more than just one integral at a time.

Finally, Ross noted to me that this integral actually has applications in physics: "this integral actually comes out of computing the time averages \(\left\langle r^n \right\rangle\) of a Kepler orbit \(r = r_0/(1+a\cos(\theta))\)."
