Skinny Repeal

Jul 27, 2017

Today, Republicans announced the Health Care Freedom Act, notably removing the individual and employer mandates from the Affordable Care Act. By removing the requirement for people to have insurance, Republicans tout this bill as restoring choice to the American consumer, thereby reducing healthcare prices. This sounds like music in a speech: health insurance providers still can't discriminate against people with pre-existing conditions, but now consumers are not forced to pay for insurance they don't want! This seems like a double-win for the American people.

Here's the catch: not only will a skinny repeal not lower costs, it's actually much worse than having Obamacare repealed entirely. I wish I had more time to do more research and flesh out all the statistics and details, since health care is such a crucial topic in today's age, but our government, so insistent on pushing repeal through as a political move, compelled a quick response.

Obamacare's monumental achievement was to lower healh care costs to the most vulnerable. Some people are at greater risk of disease than others: this has held true in the past and will continue to be true for the foreseeable future. People with different genetics, different lifestyles, and different previous illnesses face different likelihoods of falling ill in the future. We can get a sense of consumer behavior through a simple model: at period 1, say one half of the population has a 20% chance and the other half of the population has an 80% chance of falling ill in period 2. Let's also suppose that, without insurance, a consumer pays $0 when healthy and $1000 when ill in period 2.

Pre-Obamacare, people with the highest risk faced the highest insurance premiums, and people with the lowest risk faced the lowest. Insurance is not inherently a transfer from the low-risk to the high-risk; it is a means for each individual person to hedge their bets. Almost everyone is risk-averse to some degree: given a choice between $500 guaranteed or a 50% chance at $1000, I think most people would choose the guaranteed money. Insurance is precisely this. An insurance company, by aggregating independent probabilities, can stabilize risk for everyone. Before the Affordable Care Act, the fair insurance rate for low-risk individuals is to pay $200 and for high-risk individuals to pay $800 in period 1. Because individuals are risk-averse, both low- and high-risk individuals are happy with this plan. By discrimination, insurance companies can set fair prices.

The problem here is that oftentimes individuals find themselves at a high risk of disease at no fault of their own because of bad luck previously in life or even before birth. A core idea behind Obamacare is to share health care costs across all individuals, irrespective of high or low risk levels. Democrats justified this with a plea for fairness: it's largely not a high-risk individual's fault that they are more likely to fall ill. Republicans oppose this also on the grounds of fairness: it's not the responsibility of the low-risk individual to have to pay for the high-risk individual's bad luck, either. My purpose here is not to convince the reader one way or another on this issue, but merely that skinny repeal is a strictly worse option than both.

Post-Obamacare, insurance companies can no longer discriminate on pre-existing conditions. In our model, this means that insurance companies set a single price for both high- and low-risk individuals. However, Obamacare also includes an individual mandate: all individuals must buy insurance. Knowing that all individuals must buy insurance, companies will set the fair price of $500 for everyone. This is a great deal for high-risk individuals, since they are now paying much lower than they would have without Obamacare. Low-risk individuals now must pay more than they are willing, but they must buy the insurance anyway to subsidize the high-risk individuals' health care. Behind the veil of ignorance, prior to knowing risk level, paying $500 is perfectly fair.

The trouble comes when we remove the individual mandate, but leave insurance companies unable to discriminate. Let's suppose the insurance company sets its price at $500 as before. The high-risk individuals will all buy this plan, but none of the low-risk individuals will buy it. In this scenario, the insurance company will lose money, so it will certainly not set its price at $500, or indeed, any price less than $800.

Then the price settles at $800, high-risk individuals will pay the same amount as without Obamacare, but low-risk individuals will be left entirely uninsured, which is a strictly worse outcome than without Obamacare at all assuming risk-aversion.

The results are even worse with more complicated models than my simple two-state, two-period example. In the real world, people have all different probabilities of falling ill. It follows fairly easily from the simple model that insurance companies will only charge the fair price of the individuals at greatest risk, since at any other price, they will lose money. As we spread more and more people across this continuum of illness risk, we see that more and more people are left without access to insurance.

Banning price discrimination without an individual mandate, then, is essentially a ban on health care insurance entirely. Despite making for a great sound bite, skinny repeal is an incredibly irresponsible and damaging political maneuver, and if it is enacted, skinny repeal is far, far worse for the American people than full repeal.
