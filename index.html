sicut cor ita cervus

Oct 30, 2017

I knew I belonged in Harvard Glee Club when we sang a TTBB arrangement of Palestrina's Sicut cervus at the first rehearsal freshman year. The text is an adaptation of Psalm 42: Sicut cervus desiderat ad fontes aquarum, ita desiderat anima mea ad te, Deus. The sheet music we were using had a typo in the English translation, using heart instead of hart as the translation for cervus. Interestingly, just as hart and heart sound similar, so do cervus and the Latin word for heart, cor, and even more interestingly, this is not a coincidence.

Skinny Repeal

Jul 27, 2017

Today, Republicans announced the Health Care Freedom Act, notably removing the individual and employer mandates from the Affordable Care Act. By removing the requirement for people to have insurance, Republicans tout this bill as restoring choice to the American consumer, thereby reducing healthcare prices. This sounds like music in a speech: health insurance providers still can't discriminate against people with pre-existing conditions, but now consumers are not forced to pay for insurance they don't want! This seems like a double-win for the American people.

Here's the catch: not only will a skinny repeal not lower costs, it's actually much worse than having Obamacare repealed entirely.

End-of-Life Agency

Jul 26, 2017

To what extent should people be able to control their futures, and to what extent can the government take that away? Last week, Jacob and I were discussing whether the government has done too little or too much in shaping our life decisions. The United States gives its citizens plenty of agency in weighing the short and long term. I can choose what food I eat and how much I exercise, as it's entirely my decision whether having a satisfying but unhealthy lifestyle is worth shaving years off the end of my life. I assess risk constantly, from inching closer to the edge of a cliff for that perfect photo to simply biking to and from work, internalizing the probability that I get into an accident. I can choose my career: some jobs are more financially risky while some are physically more dangerous, some better fit my skill set while others I find more intrinsically appealling, but the government leaves all of these factors up to the market to set prices and wages, and from there I make my own choice.
